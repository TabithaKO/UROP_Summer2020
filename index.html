SUMMARY

Understanding how the brain works requires the study of natural behavior. In rodents, complex behaviors can be tracked using videography. Tracking behaviors in video data manually is time consuming and subject to researcher bias. In this project, we explored the feasibility of tracking animal behavior in an automated, unbiased manner with computer vision. We collected video of a freely moving rat in an open arena and then used markerless pose prediction software, DeepLabCut (DLC), to automatically track the features of a freely behaving rat. We explored other pose prediction software packages such as Deep Pose Kit and Animal Parts Tracker, but had best results with DLC.

The results obtained from the pose prediction algorithm were then used in a forecasting model that predicted the future trajectory of the rat for up to 2 seconds. These predicted trajectories can be compared with the machine generated trajectories to validate normal movement patterns or identify movement anomalies. One of the other applications of the pose prediction results is behavior classification using unsupervised deep learning methods. Utilizing data from DLC for unsupervised learning can be accomplished using software tools like MoSeq for behavior classification.

CURIOSITY

Lesson: Uncertainty is part of the job, but try anyway.

How it began ...

In February 2020, I walked up to my professor at a NSBE networking event and said:

"Hi. My name is Tabitha. I'm a sophomore in Computer Science. I taught myself machine learning and I'm looking for an opportunity to use this knowledge on some real-world applications. Do you have any openings at your lab?

He said yes and later on gave me more context on the work he and his team do at the lab.

I got this research opportunity because I was willing to try, not because I had an impressive resume. I was willing to work with my mentor and learn as much as I possible could.

My professor is in Biomedical Engineering and I'm in Computer Science. At BU those 2 are not even in the same college. We discuss Neuroscience related research papers on a weekly basis. I understand about 30% of the content, but that's ok because I get to ask questions whenever stuff is unclear. What's imprtant is that I never ask the same question twice.

I was at the lab for 10 weeks over the summer and learnt way more than i bargained for. The main takeaways are:

  • Keep asking questions. (even the 'dumb' ones).
  • Keep showing up. (you never know who you'll encounter).
  • Keep reaching out to people you want to work with. The right person will respond.
  • Don't be afraid to step out of your comfort zone (I disliked biology in high school, who'd have thought I'd work with a biomedical engineering professor)
  • Remember: You're already a winner for trying!

DILIGENCE

Lesson: It's the tasks that you assign yourself that matter the most.

My mentor and I would often begin the week by discussing a few objectives and the methods required to achieve those objectives. There are a few times when I was going to be the first person at the lab to implement certain software, so I didn't have a reference to consult or a clear vision of the end product.

Sometimes this was scary. What if I messed up? What if I ruined the data?

The point is that there were a few tasks my mentor wanted me to complete that were known to the both of us. However, there were even more tasks that I needed to do and more information that I needed to obtain in order to do the specified tasks efficiently -- these tasks were only known to me.

What did I do?

  • I consulted Google. It was important for me to do my due diligence before reporting bugs/blockers.
  • I read research papers about the technology.
  • I tried a few simple tutorials.(There's a lot you can learn on YouTube and the TensorFlow docs)
  • I read Medium articles that simplified the information.
  • I documented all the unsuccessful methods I had tried in order to track my progress.

My mentor didn't need to know all the things that I did behind the scenes, but my project benefitted from me putting in the extra work.

FLEXIBILITY

Lesson: Prototypes fail and the hypothesis gets vague, but you've got to keep moving.

One of the new realizations I got while working at the lab is that I often get to be my own student, teacher, and grader. This was unlike any of my college classes. When you start a project, you come up with the hypothesis and you develop the route to the solution. Finally, you know when you're right because your method validates your hypothesis.

We began by asking the question: 'Can we use deep learning to annotate animal behavior?' In order to answer this question I needed to understand how CNNs worked. It was a rude shock when I realized that my proposed route failed me a couple of times, and that my implementations of some methods were highly inefficient. It was in those moments that my mentor stepped in and suggested an alternative route to take.

We unexpectedly ended up answering the question: 'Can we forecast the future position of an animal using deep learning?' For this task I learned how to implement RNNs for forecasting.

The takeaways:

  • Be flexible with your methods
  • It's ok to ask for help (In fact it's wise)
  • Learn to communicate efficiently
  • Be open to criticism
  • It's ok to get things wrong sometimes
  • Again, don't be afraid to step out of your comfort zone
